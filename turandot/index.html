Who's Who

Six voices will carry this story. No need to memorize — the surtitles will guide you. But knowing them in advance is like recognizing faces before a party: everything clicks faster.

Princess Turandot
Dramatic Soprano
The ice princess of Peking. Beautiful, terrifying, unreachable. She poses three riddles to her suitors — fail, and you die. There's a reason she's this way, and the opera slowly peels it back.
Prince Calaf
Tenor
An exiled prince, traveling in disguise. One look at Turandot and he's done for. Brave or reckless? The opera doesn't quite decide. He sings that aria.
Liu
Soprano
A young servant who has followed Calaf's blind father through exile, sustained by a single kind smile Calaf once gave her. Her love is the quiet, selfless kind. Watch her closely.
Timur
Bass
Calaf's father, a deposed king now blind and vulnerable. His presence grounds the story in human tenderness amid all the spectacle.
Ping, Pang & Pong
Baritone & Two Tenors
The Emperor's three ministers. Comic relief with a melancholy edge — they dream of peaceful country lives far from all this courtly death. In this production: reimagined as tech-era bureaucrats.
The Emperor Altoum
Tenor
Turandot's ancient father. Weary. Wishes someone would just solve the riddles so this can all stop. You'll feel for him.

The Story (Spoiler-Lite)

I'm giving you the emotional roadmap, not the turns. The surtitles and the music will handle the rest. Trust them — Puccini was perhaps the greatest theatrical storyteller who ever wrote notes on paper.

Act I — The Law of the Land
Mood: Dread, wonder, obsession

We arrive in a Peking drenched in fear. Someone has failed Turandot's riddles — the consequences are public and horrible. The crowd is feverish. Into this walks Calaf, who spots Turandot and, against all warnings from everyone around him, decides: I will answer the riddles. The whole first act is people begging him not to. He does it anyway.

Act II — The Three Riddles
Mood: Tension, triumph, a twist

The grand scene. Turandot explains why she does what she does — a wound from generations past. Then: three riddles, each more intense than the last. This is the sequence that'll have your heart in your throat. What follows the riddles... well, expect the unexpected. Calaf makes a bold move of his own.

Interval

~20 minutes. Breathe. Grab your pre-ordered drink. Enjoy the harbour. Process what you've seen.

Act III — None Shall Sleep
Mood: Suspense, sacrifice, transformation

Night falls. Tension rises. This is where Nessun dorma lives — you'll know it when it hits. The act pushes every character to their emotional limit. There are real costs. There is real change. And at the end... well, that's between you and Puccini.

The Unfinished Masterpiece

Puccini died in 1924 before completing the final scene. At the world premiere in 1926, conductor Arturo Toscanini famously stopped the orchestra at the exact point where Puccini's manuscript ended, turned to the audience, and said: "Here the opera ends, because at this point the Maestro died."

The ending you'll hear was completed by Franco Alfano from Puccini's sketches. It's been debated for a century — some find it beautiful, some find it rushed. Listen with that knowledge: the seams between two composers, 100 years apart from you, trying to finish a love story.

Moments to Listen For

You don't need to "know" these in advance — they'll hit you regardless. But having a vague radar for them is like knowing when the drop comes in a song. You lean in.

Signore, ascolta — Liu's Plea (Act I)

Liu begs Calaf not to attempt the riddles. A heartbreaking soprano aria of pure, unguarded love. It's the emotional core many opera-lovers consider Puccini's finest writing. Delicate. Devastating.

In questa Reggia — Turandot's Entrance (Act II)

Turandot finally sings. She tells the story of her ancestor and why she refuses love. The soprano climbs to notes that are genuinely athletic. It's half aria, half battle cry.

The Riddle Scene (Act II)

Three riddles, answered one by one, each a mini-drama. The tension ratchets with each question. The orchestral score here is magnificent — Puccini builds the suspense like a thriller composer.

Nessun dorma — None Shall Sleep (Act III)

The most famous tenor aria in opera. You've heard it in movies, stadiums, commercials. But nothing — nothing — compares to hearing it live, in context, when you know what's at stake. Here are the words, so you can close your eyes and just feel it:

Nessun dorma! Nessun dorma!
None shall sleep! None shall sleep!
Tu pure, o Principessa,
nella tua fredda stanza,
guardi le stelle
che tremano d'amore
e di speranza.
Even you, oh Princess,
in your cold room,
watch the stars
that tremble with love
and with hope.
Ma il mio mistero e chiuso in me,
il nome mio nessun sapra!
But my secret is locked within me,
my name no one shall know!
No, no, sulla tua bocca
lo diro quando la luce splendera!
No, no, upon your lips
I will tell it when the light shines!
Ed il mio bacio sciogliera
il silenzio che ti fa mia!
And my kiss will dissolve
the silence that makes you mine!
Dilegua, o notte! Tramontate, stelle!
Vanish, o night! Set, you stars!
All'alba vincero! Vincero! VINCERO!
At dawn, I will win! I will win! I WILL WIN!

The Chorus

Turandot has Puccini's largest-ever choral writing. The crowd is practically a character. When they surge, the sound is physical. You'll feel it in your ribcage.

This Production

This isn't a "traditional" Turandot with pagodas and chinoiserie. Director Ann Yee (choreographer and director, Australian debut) has reimagined it as a psychological landscape, centering intergenerational trauma.

What to Expect Visually

  • Monumental modular set that moves, revolves, creates new spaces
  • A dancer (Hoyori Maruo) embodies Lou-Ling, Turandot's ancestor, in haunting silent choreography
  • A giant stylized face with moving eyes watches from upstage
  • Ping, Pang & Pong reimagined as tech-era administrators with computer banks
  • Immersive video design by Andrew Thomas Huang

The Voices

Two sopranos alternate as Turandot: Rebecca Nash (fresh from the Met in New York) and Anna-Louise Cole. Reviews call Nash's performance a "triumphant homecoming." Tenor Young Woo Kim as Calaf has been singled out across every review — his Nessun dorma described as beautifully modulated, powerful without brute force.

Maria Teresa Leva makes her Australian debut as Liu with what critics call "crystalline impeccable purity." The kind of voice that makes you forget to breathe.

Conductor

Henrik Nanasi (also Australian debut) leads the orchestra "with flair and authority," keeping the massive forces from overwhelming the singers while letting Puccini's orchestration soar.

About the 100th Anniversary

Turandot premiered on April 25, 1926 — making 2026 its centenary year. This Opera Australia production is part of a national partnership (the Opera Conference) celebrating the milestone. You're seeing a piece of history marking its own century. Not bad for a first opera.

First-Timer Survival Kit

👕
Wear What You Want

Jeans to cocktail dresses — Opera Australia literally says "wear something you feel good in, that's it." No top hats required.

⏰
Arrive 30 Min Early

7-10 min walk from Circular Quay. Grab your tickets, soak in the building, pre-order your interval drinks at the bar. They'll be waiting for you.

📖
Surtitles Have You Covered

English translations projected above the stage. You'll follow everything. No Italian required — though now you know "vincero" means "I will win."

👏
When to Clap

After arias (you'll know — big vocal moment, pause, orchestra settles). After each act. At the end. Shout "bravo" (male), "brava" (female), or "bravi" (everyone) if you feel it. Don't hold back.

📵
Phone Off, Not Vibrate

Silence is part of the art. Those quiet moments between notes — that's where the magic lives. Give them to yourself.

🎭
Let It Hit You

Don't intellectualize during the show. You prepared (you're here!). Now let go. If your eyes get wet during Liu's aria, you're doing opera right.
